## Drawings and paintings are NOT the same although Drawing skecthes can help painters know were to paint

| Features | Drawing | Painting |
|----------|---------|----------|
| Primary Medium | Dry media (pencil, charcoal, chalk, ink pens, pastel) | Wet media (oil paint, acrylic paint, watercolor, gouache, tempera) | 
Surface | Most often paper, but also canvas or wood | Most often canvas, wood panels, or specially prepared paper | 
Focus | Emphasizes line, form, tone/shading, and texture | Emphasizes color, texture, and complex composition | 
Process | Generally more immediate and spontaneous | Often more time-consuming, involving layering and blending |

## The lines between drawing and painting often blur BTW.

## Many artists use a sketch as the foundation for a painting, and some drawings, like those made with colored pencils or soft pastels, are so rich with color they feel like paintings.



# The Building Blocks: Essential Drawing Techniques
## Think of these techniques as the foundational skills for creating the illusion of form, shadow, and depth on a flat surface.

# Hatching & Cross-Hatching: Drawing with Lines

![Example of Hatching and Cross-Hatching](images/hatching.jpg "Cross Hatch Practice")

## Hatching is the use of fine, parallel lines to create shading. Cross-hatching layers another set of lines in a different direction (often perpendicular) over the first to build up darker tones. 

## The primary goal of both techniques is to create tonal values and shadow. The closer the lines are to each other, the darker the shadow appears, giving a drawing a powerful sense of three-dimensional form.

![Example of a drawing using the cross-hatching teachnique](images/cross-hatching-Example-1200-80-1080x675.jpg "Example of a drawing using the cross-hatching teachnique")

# Stippling: Building Tone with Dots
## Stippling is a method of creating tone and texture using patterns of dots.

![Stippling example](images/sddefault.jpg "Stippling example")

## Similar to hatching, stippling is used to build up tone and create unique textures. Areas with a higher concentration of dots appear darker, while sparser dots create lighter tones, allowing for soft, subtle gradients. It's a patient process, but the results can be stunningly soft and realistic.

# The World of Color & Texture: Expressive Painting Techniques
## These techniques focus on the physical qualities of the paint itself to create drama, convey emotion, and add a tangible sense of energy to the artwork.
# Chiaroscuro: The Drama of Light and Dark
## Chiaroscuro is an Italian term for the dramatic use of strong contrasts between light and dark. Its primary effect is to create a powerful sense of volume and depth in a painting, making figures seem to emerge from a dark background. This technique was famously mastered by artists like Caravaggio and Rembrandt to give their figures incredible weight and presence.

# Impasto: Painting with Texture You Can Feel
## Impasto is the technique of applying paint so thickly that it physically stands out from the surface. The individual brush strokes or even marks from a palette knife are clearly visible, making the brushwork a key part of the artwork itself. Its primary effect is to create visible texture and dimension, adding an expressive energy to the painting.
## Artists like Van Gogh favored the Impasto technique to convey intense emotion and energy in his work. These are just a few of the many powerful tools an artist can use to bring their vision to life.
